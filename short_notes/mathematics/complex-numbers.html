The main path: complex number → polar form → complex function → limit → continuity → differentiability → analyticity. Each step builds on the previous one, and the Cauchy–Riemann equations are the centrepiece.

Complex Numbers — Geometric View

A complex number is both a number and a point in the Argand plane. Arithmetic becomes geometry: addition is vector addition; multiplication is rotation combined with scaling.

Definition — Complex Number
$z=x+iy$ where $x=\operatorname{Re}z$, $y=\operatorname{Im}z$, $i^2=-1$. The modulus is $|z|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ and the conjugate is $\bar{z}=x-iy$. Key: $z\bar{z}=|z|^2$.
De Moivre's Theorem
$(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^n=\cos(n\theta)+i\sin(n\theta)$ for any integer $n$. In polar form: $(re^{i\theta})^n=r^n e^{in\theta}$.
Intuition
Multiplying two complex numbers: multiply the moduli, add the arguments. So $z^n$ has modulus $|z|^n$ and argument $n\arg z$.

Euler's Formula

Euler's formula connects the exponential function to trigonometry through the imaginary unit. It is the key that unlocks polar form, roots, and complex powers.

Theorem — Euler's Formula
$$e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta$$ Multiplying a complex number by $e^{i\theta}$ rotates it by angle $\theta$ counterclockwise. Euler's identity: $e^{i\pi}+1=0$.
$e^{i\theta}=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(i\theta)^n}{n!}=1+i\theta-\frac{\theta^2}{2!}-\frac{i\theta^3}{3!}+\frac{\theta^4}{4!}+\cdots$

Real part: $1-\theta^2/2!+\theta^4/4!-\cdots=\cos\theta$. Imaginary part: $\theta-\theta^3/3!+\cdots=\sin\theta$.

Polar Representation

Polar form is ideal for multiplication, division, and powers. Every complex number can be written as modulus times a unit rotation.

Definition — Polar Form
$$z=\rho e^{i\theta}=\rho(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)$$ $\rho=|z|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$, $x=\rho\cos\theta$, $y=\rho\sin\theta$, $\theta=\arg z$.
FormBest for
$x+iy$ (Cartesian)Addition, subtraction, finding real/imaginary parts
$\rho(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)$Geometric intuition
$\rho e^{i\theta}$ (exponential)Powers, roots, multiplication, division
Multiplication and Division
$$z_1 z_2=\rho_1\rho_2\,e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)}\qquad\frac{z_1}{z_2}=\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_2}\,e^{i(\theta_1-\theta_2)}$$
Quadrant check
$\theta=\arctan(y/x)$ returns values in $(-\pi/2,\pi/2)$ only. Always check the signs of $x$ and $y$ to determine the correct quadrant before stating $\arg z$.

Roots of Unity

The $n$-th roots of unity are the $n$ solutions of $z^n=1$. They form a regular $n$-gon on the unit circle, equally spaced by angle $2\pi/n$.

Theorem — $n$-th Roots
Solutions of $z^n=w$ where $w=|w|e^{i\phi}$: $$z_k=|w|^{1/n}\,e^{i(\phi+2\pi k)/n},\quad k=0,1,\ldots,n-1$$ There are exactly $n$ roots, all with modulus $|w|^{1/n}$, spaced $2\pi/n$ apart in argument.
Intuition
Raising to the $n$-th power multiplies the argument by $n$. To "undo" this, divide the argument by $n$ — but you can add any multiple of $2\pi$ before dividing, giving $n$ distinct roots.

Complex Functions

A complex function $f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y)$ maps one complex plane to another. Writing it in terms of real and imaginary parts is essential for applying the Cauchy–Riemann equations.

Definition — $u$ and $v$ parts
$f:D\to\mathbb{C}$ where $D\subseteq\mathbb{C}$. With $z=x+iy$: $f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y)$. The partial derivatives $u_x,u_y,v_x,v_y$ appear in the CR equations.
Example — splitting $f(z)=z^2-z+2$

With $z=x+iy$: $z^2=x^2-y^2+2ixy$. So $f(z)=(x^2-y^2-x+2)+i(2xy-y)$.

$$u=x^2-y^2-x+2,\quad v=2xy-y$$

Then $u_x=2x-1$, $u_y=-2y$, $v_x=2y$, $v_y=2x-1$. Check CR: $u_x=v_y=2x-1$ ✓; $u_y=-2y=-v_x$ ✓. So $f$ is analytic everywhere.

Limits of Complex Functions

Complex limits are strictly harder than real limits: $z$ can approach $z_0$ from any direction in the plane. The limit only exists if the value is the same along every path.

Definition — Complex Limit
$\displaystyle\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=L$ means: for every $\varepsilon>0$, $\exists\delta>0$ such that $0<|z-z_0|<\delta\Rightarrow|f(z)-L|<\varepsilon$.
Two-Path Test — Disproving Limits
If two different paths to $z_0$ give different values for $f$, the limit does not exist.
Standard paths to try: $y=0$ (real axis), $x=0$ (imaginary axis), $y=mx$, $y=x^2$, polar $z=re^{i\theta}$ (check if result depends on $\theta$).
Path 1 (real axis, $z=x$, $x\to 0$): $\bar{z}/z=x/x=1$.

Path 2 (imaginary axis, $z=iy$, $y\to 0$): $\bar{z}/z=-iy/iy=-1$.

Two paths give different values → limit does not exist.
Bounding Technique — Proving Limits
To prove $\displaystyle\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=L$: find an estimate $|f(z)-L|\le C|z-z_0|^k$ with $k>0$. Since $|z-z_0|\to 0$, the bound forces $|f(z)-L|\to 0$.
$|z\bar{z}-0|=|z||\bar{z}|=|z|^2$. With $z=re^{i\theta}$, $|z\bar{z}|=r^2\to 0$ as $r\to 0$. ✓
$\left|\frac{x^2y}{x^2+y^2}\right|=\frac{|x|^2|y|}{x^2+y^2}\le\frac{x^2+y^2}{x^2+y^2}\cdot|y|=|y|\le|z|.$ Since $|z|\to 0$, the limit exists and equals 0.
Limits at infinity & useful estimates

$|1/\bar{z}|=1/|z|\to 0$ as $|z|\to\infty$, so $\lim_{|z|\to\infty}1/\bar{z}=0$.

Near $z=0$: $|1/z^3|=1/|z|^3\to\infty$, so no finite limit exists at 0.

$$\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=L\iff\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)}u(x,y)=\operatorname{Re}(L)\;\text{ and }\;\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)}v(x,y)=\operatorname{Im}(L)$$

Continuity

$f$ is continuous at $z_0$ exactly when the limit exists and equals the function value — the same three conditions as in real analysis, but now for the complex plane.

Definition — Continuity
$f$ is continuous at $z_0$ if: (i) $f(z_0)$ is defined; (ii) $\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)$ exists; (iii) $\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=f(z_0)$. Equivalently, $u$ and $v$ are both continuous at $(x_0,y_0)$.
Function typeContinuous where
Polynomial in $z$All of $\mathbb{C}$
Rational $p(z)/q(z)$$\mathbb{C}\setminus\{z:q(z)=0\}$
$e^z$, $\sin z$, $\cos z$All of $\mathbb{C}$ (entire)
$\ln z$ (principal branch)$\mathbb{C}\setminus(-\infty,0]$
Rational function — continuous where denominator $\ne 0$. Solve $z^2+9=0$: $z=\pm 3i$. So $f$ is continuous on $\mathbb{C}\setminus\{3i,-3i\}$.
Example — Im(z) is continuous everywhere

$\operatorname{Im}(z)=y$. If $z\to z_0=a+ib$, then $y\to b=\operatorname{Im}(z_0)$. So $\operatorname{Im}(z)$ is continuous everywhere — as are $\operatorname{Re}(z)$, $|z|$, and $\bar{z}$.

Differentiability

Complex differentiability is far more restrictive than real differentiability because the difference quotient must give the same value for every direction of approach.

Definition — Complex Derivative
$$f'(z_0)=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}$$ This limit (if it exists) must be the same for every direction in which $\Delta z\to 0$.
Standard Derivative Rules
The real rules carry over: $(z^n)'=nz^{n-1}$; $(e^z)'=e^z$; $(\sin z)'=\cos z$; $(\cos z)'=-\sin z$; chain rule, product rule, quotient rule all apply.
Key contrast with real analysis
$f(z)=\bar{z}$ is continuous everywhere but differentiable nowhere. $f(z)=|z|^2$ is differentiable at $z=0$ only but not analytic anywhere. Complex differentiability is a global neighbourhood property.

Analytic Functions

Analyticity means differentiability throughout an open neighbourhood — not just at one isolated point. This distinction is the central subtlety of complex analysis.

Definition — Analytic
$f$ is analytic at $z_0$ if it is differentiable at every point in some open disc around $z_0$. $f$ is analytic on a domain $D$ if it is analytic at every point of $D$.
Analyticity vs differentiability at one point
Analyticity is strictly stronger: you can be differentiable at one isolated point without being analytic there.
Example — $|z|^2$ is differentiable at 0, not analytic at 0

$f(z)=|z|^2=x^2+y^2$, so $u=x^2+y^2$, $v=0$. CR equations: $u_x=2x$, $v_y=0$, $u_y=2y$, $v_x=0$. These hold only at $(0,0)$.

So $f$ is differentiable only at $z=0$ (with $f'(0)=0$), not in any neighbourhood of $0$ → not analytic at $0$.

Cauchy–Riemann Equations

The CR equations are the algebraic signature of complex differentiability. They are necessary; with continuous partial derivatives they are also sufficient.

Theorem — Cauchy–Riemann Equations
If $f=u+iv$ is differentiable at $z_0$, then at $(x_0,y_0)$: $$\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\qquad\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=-\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}$$ Moreover $f'(z_0)=u_x+iv_x=v_y-iu_y$.
Approach along real axis ($\Delta z=h$ real): $f'(z_0)=u_x+iv_x$.

Approach along imaginary axis ($\Delta z=ik$): $f'(z_0)=v_y-iu_y$.

Setting equal: real parts give $u_x=v_y$; imaginary parts give $v_x=-u_y$.
Theorem — Sufficient Conditions (CR → Analytic)
If $u_x,u_y,v_x,v_y$ exist, are continuous throughout a neighbourhood of $z_0$, and satisfy the CR equations there, then $f$ is analytic on that neighbourhood.
Intuition
The CR equations enforce that the local stretching/rotation is the same from every direction. Without them, different directions of approach give contradictory derivatives.

Cauchy–Riemann in practice
  • If CR equations fail at a point, the function is not differentiable there.
  • If CR equations hold and partial derivatives are continuous in a region → analytic.
  • $f(z)=\bar{z}$: $u_x=1\ne -1=v_y$ everywhere → nowhere analytic.
  • $f(z)=e^z=e^x\cos y+ie^x\sin y$: $u_x=e^x\cos y=v_y$, $u_y=-e^x\sin y=-v_x$ → entire.
  • $f(z)=|z|^2$: CR holds only at $(0,0)$ → differentiable at 0 but nowhere analytic.

Entire Functions

An entire function is analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$. These are the "nicest" complex functions — their CR equations hold everywhere with continuous partials.

Definition — Entire Function
$f$ is entire if it is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$. The set of entire functions is closed under addition, multiplication, and composition.
