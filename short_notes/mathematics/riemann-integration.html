Overview

The Riemann integral formalises the idea of signed area under a curve. For a bounded function $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ we ask: can we assign a well-defined number to the area? The answer is yes — by squeezing upper and lower rectangular approximations until they agree.

The Riemann integral of $f$ on $[a,b]$ is the unique value squeezed between every lower and upper sum:

$L(f,P) \;\le\; \displaystyle\int_a^b f \;\le\; U(f,P)$

defined when $\sup_P L(f,P) = \inf_P U(f,P)$

Partitions & Riemann Sums

We slice $[a,b]$ into sub-intervals, build rectangles above and below the curve, then take the inf/sup over all possible slicings.

Definition — Partition
A partition $P$ of $[a,b]$ is a finite set of almost-disjoint sub-intervals $\{I_1,\dots,I_n\}$ with $\bigcup I_k=[a,b]$, determined by endpoints $a=x_0\lt x_1\lt\cdots\lt x_n=b$.
Definition — Upper & Lower Sums
With $M_k=\sup_{I_k}f$ and $m_k=\inf_{I_k}f$: $$U(f;P)=\sum_{k=1}^n M_k|I_k|, \qquad L(f;P)=\sum_{k=1}^n m_k|I_k|$$

Lemma 0.2.1
For any bounded $f$ on $[a,b]$ and any partition $P$: $\;L(f,P)\le U(f,P)$.

Refinements

$Q$ is a refinement of $P$ if $P\subseteq Q$ (more points added). Adding points can only improve the approximation.

Lemma 0.2.2 — Refinement
If $Q$ is a refinement of $P$: $\;L(f,P)\le L(f,Q)\le U(f,Q)\le U(f,P)$.
Intuition
Adding partition points tightens the rectangles. Lower sums can only increase; upper sums can only decrease.
Lemma 0.2.3
For any two partitions $P_1, P_2$: $\;L(f,P_1)\le U(f,P_2)$.
Let $Q=P_1\cup P_2$ (common refinement of both). Then $L(f,P_1)\le L(f,Q)\le U(f,Q)\le U(f,P_2)$.

Riemann Integrability

The upper integral is the best (lowest) upper estimate; the lower integral the best (highest) lower estimate. Integrability means these two estimates agree.

Definition — Upper & Lower Integrals
$$U(f)=\inf_P U(f,P), \qquad L(f)=\sup_P L(f,P)$$
Lemma 0.2.4
For any bounded $f$ on $[a,b]$: $\;L(f)\le U(f)$.
Definition — Riemann Integrable
A bounded function $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ is Riemann integrable if $U(f)=L(f)$. In that case: $$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx \;=\; U(f) = L(f)$$
Intuition
The signed area exists exactly when the upper and lower rectangles can be made arbitrarily close — there's no ambiguity in the area value.

Theorem 0.3.5 — Sequential Characterisation
$f$ is Riemann integrable iff there exists a sequence of partitions $(P_n)$ with $\lim_{n\to\infty}[U(f;P_n)-L(f;P_n)]=0$. In that case $\int_a^b f=\lim U(f;P_n)=\lim L(f;P_n)$.
Extra integrability facts
  • If \(f\) is Riemann integrable, then \(|f|\) is Riemann integrable.
  • If \(f\) is Riemann integrable, then \(f^n\) is Riemann integrable for every positive integer \(n\).
  • Changing a bounded function at finitely many points does not affect Riemann integrability or the value of the integral.

Example: on \([0,m]\), a function equal to \(1\) except at the finitely many integers is Riemann integrable and has the same integral as \(1\).

Thomae's function

Thomae's function is

\[ f(x)= \begin{cases} 1/q,& x=p/q \text{ in lowest terms},\\ 0,& x \text{ irrational}. \end{cases} \]

It is discontinuous at rationals, continuous at irrationals, Riemann integrable on \([0,1]\), and

\[ \boxed{\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx=0.} \]

Cauchy Criterion & Integrability Theorems

A practical test for integrability: produce one partition where upper and lower sums are within $\varepsilon$ of each other.

Theorem 0.2.1 — Cauchy Criterion
$f$ is Riemann integrable $\iff$ for every $\varepsilon\gt 0$ there exists a partition $P$ such that $U(f,P)-L(f,P)\le\varepsilon$.
($\Leftarrow$) If such $P$ exists: $U(f)-L(f)\le U(f,P)-L(f,P)\lt\varepsilon$. Since $\varepsilon$ is arbitrary, $U(f)=L(f)$.

($\Rightarrow$) If $f$ is integrable, given $\varepsilon\gt 0$ choose $P_1,P_2$ with $L(f,P_1)\gt L(f)-\varepsilon/2$ and $U(f,P_2)\lt U(f)+\varepsilon/2$. Let $P=P_1\cup P_2$. Then $U(f,P)-L(f,P)\le U(f,P_2)-L(f,P_1)\lt\varepsilon$.
Theorem 0.2.2 — Monotone → Integrable
Every monotone function $f$ on $[a,b]$ is Riemann integrable.
For increasing $f$, choose equal sub-intervals of width $\delta=\varepsilon/[f(b)-f(a)]$. Then $M_k-m_k=f(x_k)-f(x_{k-1})$ (telescoping). So $U-L=\sum[f(x_k)-f(x_{k-1})]\cdot\delta=[f(b)-f(a)]\cdot\delta=\varepsilon$.
Theorem 0.2.3 — Continuous → Integrable
If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then $f$ is Riemann integrable.
Continuous on compact $[a,b]$ $\Rightarrow$ uniformly continuous. Given $\varepsilon\gt 0$, choose $\delta$ so $|x-y|\lt\delta\Rightarrow|f(x)-f(y)|\lt\varepsilon/[2(b-a)]$. Partition with $\Delta x_k\lt\delta$. Then $M_k-m_k\lt\varepsilon/[2(b-a)]$, giving $U-L\lt\varepsilon$.
Theorem 0.2.4 — Near-Endpoint Integrability
If $f$ is bounded on $[a,b]$ and integrable on $[c,b]$ for every $c\in(a,b)$, then $f$ is integrable on $[a,b]$.
Theorem 0.2.6 — Splitting Integrability
If $f$ is integrable on $[a,b]$ and $[b,c]$, then it is integrable on $[a,c]$.
Key Note
A function with finitely many discontinuities is Riemann integrable. Even infinitely many discontinuities are OK if their set of limit points is finite.
Upper/lower sums for \(-f\)

For every partition \(P\),

\[ U(-f,P)=-L(f,P), \qquad L(-f,P)=-U(f,P). \]

Reason: on each subinterval, \(\sup(-f)=-\inf(f)\) and \(\inf(-f)=-\sup(f)\).

Properties of the Integral

The Riemann integral behaves as expected: it respects addition, scaling, ordering, and can be split across sub-intervals.

Theorem 0.3.1 — Properties
For integrable $f,g$ on $[a,b]$:
  1. Linearity: $\int_a^b(f+g)=\int_a^bf+\int_a^bg$
  2. Scalar: $\int_a^b kf=k\int_a^bf$
  3. Bounds: if $m\le f\le M$ then $m(b-a)\le\int_a^bf\le M(b-a)$
  4. Monotonicity: $f\le g\;\Rightarrow\;\int_a^bf\le\int_a^bg$
  5. Triangle inequality: $\left|\int_a^bf\right|\le\int_a^b|f|$
Theorem 0.3.2
If $f,g$ are Riemann integrable, then $fg$ is also Riemann integrable.
First show $f^2$ is integrable: $U(f^2,P)-L(f^2,P)=\sum(M_j^2-m_j^2)|I_j|=\sum(M_j+m_j)(M_j-m_j)|I_j|\le 2k\sum(M_j-m_j)|I_j|\lt\varepsilon$. Then use $fg=[(f+g)^2-f^2-g^2]/2$.
Theorem 0.3.3
If $f,g$ are Riemann integrable, then $\max(f,g)$ and $\min(f,g)$ are Riemann integrable.
$\max(f,g)=\frac{|f-g|+f+g}{2}$ and $\min(f,g)=\frac{-|f-g|+f+g}{2}$. Since $f,g$ integrable, $|f-g|$ is integrable (shown similarly to $|f|$ above), so these are integrable.
Theorem 0.3.4 — Splitting
$f$ is integrable on $[a,b]$ $\iff$ it is integrable on $[a,c]$ and $[c,b]$ for any $c\in(a,b)$. In that case: $\int_a^b f=\int_a^c f+\int_c^b f$.
($\Rightarrow$) Insert $c$ into partition $P$ to get $P'$. Splitting $P'$ at $c$ gives partitions of $[a,c]$ and $[c,b]$ each satisfying $U-L\lt\varepsilon$.

($\Leftarrow$) Take partitions $P_1,P_2$ of $[a,c]$ and $[c,b]$ with $U-L\lt\varepsilon/2$ each. Their union is a partition of $[a,b]$ with $U-L\lt\varepsilon$. The additive formula follows by squeezing inequalities.

Mean Value Theorem for Integrals

A continuous function must achieve its own average value at least once. The IVT delivers the point; the name "MVT" reflects that the average (mean) value is what's being hit.

Theorem 0.4.1 — MVT for Integrals
If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then there exists $x\in(a,b)$ such that: $$f(x)=\frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f$$
Intuition
The quantity $\frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f$ is the average value of $f$. Continuity + compactness (EVT) gives actual min $m$ and max $M$. The average is between them, so by IVT the function must hit it.
Let $m,M$ be min and max of $f$ on $[a,b]$ (exist by EVT). If $m=M$, $f$ is constant and the result is trivial. Otherwise $m\lt M$ and $m\lt\frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f\lt M$. By IVT, $f$ takes every value between $m$ and $M$, including the average.

Theorem 0.4.2 — Generalised MVT
If $f,g:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ are continuous and $g(t)\ge 0$, then there exists $x\in[a,b]$ such that: $$\int_a^b f(t)g(t)\,dt = f(x)\int_a^b g(t)\,dt$$
Intuition
$g$ acts as a weight function. The ratio $\frac{\int fg}{\int g}$ is a weighted average of $f$, which $f$ must attain by IVT. Setting $g=1$ recovers the standard MVT.
Let $m,M$ be extremes of $f$. Since $g\ge 0$: $m\int g\le\int fg\le M\int g$, giving $m\le\frac{\int fg}{\int g}\le M$. By IVT, $f$ takes this value at some $x\in[a,b]$.

Sequences of Functions

When can we swap a limit and an integral? Pointwise convergence is not enough — the spike counterexample shows area can survive as the function vanishes. We need extra conditions.

The Core Question
When is $\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_a^b f_n(x)\,dx = \int_a^b \lim_{n\to\infty}f_n(x)\,dx$?

The Spike Pathology

Define $f_n=n\cdot\mathbf{1}_{[0,1/n]}$. Then $f_n\to 0$ pointwise on $(0,1]$, yet $\int f_n=1$ for all $n$. Area is preserved as the spike gets taller and thinner — it escapes to the boundary.

Theorem 0.4.3 — Uniform Convergence
If $(f_n)$ is a sequence of Riemann integrable functions on $[a,b]$ and $f_n\to f$ uniformly, then $f$ is integrable and: $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_a^b f_n(x)\,dx=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$
Why It Works
Uniform convergence forces the entire function inside an $\varepsilon$-band of $f$ simultaneously. The spike can never do this — near $x=0$ it always escapes. The band bounds the area difference: $\left|\int f_n-\int f\right|\le\int|f_n-f|\le\varepsilon(b-a)$.
Since $f_n\to f$ uniformly, $\exists N: |f_n(x)-f(x)|\lt\varepsilon/[2(b-a)]$ for all $x$ when $n\ge N$. This bounds $U(f-f_N,P)-L(f-f_N,P)\lt\varepsilon$, so $f-f_N$ is integrable, hence $f=(f-f_N)+f_N$ is integrable. For $n\ge N$: $\left|\int f_n-\int f\right|=\left|\int(f_n-f)\right|\le\int|f_n-f|\le\varepsilon/2$.
Theorem 0.4.4 — Dominated Convergence
If $f_n\to f$ pointwise on $[a,b]$ where $f$ is integrable, and $|f_n(x)|\le M$ for all $n,x$, then: $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_a^b f_n = \int_a^b f$$
Intuition
The bound $M$ acts as a ceiling and floor. Functions can't escape to infinity, so no area leaks out — even without uniform convergence.
Theorem 0.4.5 — Monotone Convergence
If $f_1\le f_2\le\cdots$ are integrable on $[a,b]$ and $f_n\to f$ pointwise where $f$ is integrable, then: $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_a^b f_n=\int_a^b f$$
Intuition
Monotone stacking means functions can only grow — area monotonically approaches the limit. MCT follows from DCT since the monotone bound gives $|f_n|\le\max(|f_1|,|f|)\le M$.
Pointwise convergence warning

For Riemann integration, pointwise convergence plus a uniform bound does not by itself guarantee

\[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\int_a^b f_n=\int_a^b \lim_{n\to\infty}f_n. \]

The limit function may fail to be Riemann integrable. Uniform convergence is the safe condition used in this course.

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The two parts say that differentiation and integration are inverse operations. FTC I turns an integral into a difference; FTC II turns an area accumulator into the original function.

Theorem 0.5.1 — FTC I
If $g$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$, and $g'$ is integrable, then: $$\int_a^b g'(x)\,dx = g(b)-g(a)$$
Intuition
The MVT on each sub-interval gives $g(x_k)-g(x_{k-1})=(x_k-x_{k-1})g'(c_k)$. Summing telescopes to $g(b)-g(a)$, which is squeezed between $L(g',P)$ and $U(g',P)$.
For any $\varepsilon\gt 0$, take partition $P$ with $U(g',P)-L(g',P)\lt\varepsilon$. Apply MVT to each $[x_{k-1},x_k]$: $g(x_k)-g(x_{k-1})=(x_k-x_{k-1})g'(c_k)$. Summing: $g(b)-g(a)=\sum(x_k-x_{k-1})g'(c_k)$, which lies between $L(g',P)$ and $U(g',P)$, hence within $\varepsilon$ of $\int_a^b g'$. Since $\varepsilon$ is arbitrary, equality holds.
Theorem 0.5.2 — Integration by Parts
If $u,v$ are continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$, and $u',v'$ integrable: $$\int_a^b u(x)v'(x)\,dx + \int_a^b u'(x)v(x)\,dx = u(b)v(b)-u(a)v(a)$$
Let $g=uv$. Then $g'=uv'+u'v$ is integrable. Apply FTC I to $g$: $\int_a^b(uv'+u'v)=g(b)-g(a)=u(b)v(b)-u(a)v(a)$. Split the integral by linearity.
Theorem 0.5.3 — FTC II
Let $f$ be integrable on $[a,b]$ and define $F(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt$. Then $F$ is continuous on $[a,b]$. If $f$ is continuous at $x_0\in(a,b)$, then $F$ is differentiable at $x_0$ and: $$F'(x_0)=f(x_0)$$
Intuition
$F(x+h)-F(x)$ is a thin sliver of area $\approx f(x)\cdot h$. Dividing by $h$ gives $f(x)$. The rate at which area accumulates equals the height of the function.
Continuity: $|F(y)-F(x)|=|\int_x^y f|\le M|y-x|$ where $|f|\le M$. So $|F(y)-F(x)|\lt\varepsilon$ when $|y-x|\lt\varepsilon/M$.

Differentiability: $\frac{F(x)-F(x_0)}{x-x_0}-f(x_0)=\frac{1}{x-x_0}\int_{x_0}^x[f(t)-f(x_0)]\,dt$. Given $\varepsilon\gt 0$, continuity of $f$ at $x_0$ gives $\delta$ with $|t-x_0|\lt\delta\Rightarrow|f(t)-f(x_0)|\lt\varepsilon$. For $|x-x_0|\lt\delta$: the difference quotient minus $f(x_0)$ has absolute value $\lt\varepsilon$.

Theorem 0.5.4 — Chain Rule Form
If $f$ is continuous on an interval containing the range of differentiable $a(x),b(x)$: $$\frac{d}{dx}\int_{a(x)}^{b(x)}f(t)\,dt = f(b(x))\cdot b'(x)-f(a(x))\cdot a'(x)$$

If $u$ is differentiable on $J$ with $u'\in C$, $f$ is continuous on $I$ with $u(J)\subseteq I$: $$\int_a^b f(u(x))\,u'(x)\,dx = \int_{u(a)}^{u(b)} f(u)\,du$$

Improper Integrals

When $f$ is unbounded or the interval is infinite, we extend the Riemann integral by taking limits. The integral converges if the limit is finite.

Definition — Type I (Unbounded Function)
If $f:(a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ is integrable on $[c,b]$ for every $c\in(a,b)$: $$\int_a^b f = \lim_{\varepsilon\to 0^+}\int_{a+\varepsilon}^b f$$
Definition — Type II (Infinite Interval)
If $f:[a,\infty)\to\mathbb{R}$ is integrable on $[a,r]$ for every $r\gt a$: $$\int_a^\infty f = \lim_{r\to\infty}\int_a^r f$$

Convergence Tests

Theorem 0.6.1 — Absolute Convergence
If $\int_a^\infty|f|\,dx\lt\infty$ (absolutely convergent), then $\int_a^\infty f\,dx$ converges.
Intuition
Absolute convergence means the function decays fast regardless of sign. Conditional convergence relies on cancellation between positive/negative parts — like $\int_1^\infty\frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx$.
Write $f^+=\frac{f+|f|}{2}\ge 0$ and $f^-=\frac{|f|-f}{2}\ge 0$. Then $f=f^+-f^-$ and $f^+,f^-\le|f|$. Since $\int|f|\lt\infty$, both $\int f^+$ and $\int f^-$ converge by comparison, so $\int f=\int f^+-\int f^-$ converges.
Theorem 0.6.2 — Comparison Theorem
If $0\le g(x)\le f(x)$ for all $x\ge a$, then:
  • $\int_a^\infty f\,dx$ converges $\;\Rightarrow\;$ $\int_a^\infty g\,dx$ converges
  • $\int_a^\infty g\,dx$ diverges $\;\Rightarrow\;$ $\int_a^\infty f\,dx$ diverges
Intuition
If the bigger function fits in the bucket, the smaller one certainly does. If the smaller function overflows, the bigger one definitely overflows.
Theorem 0.6.3 — Limit Comparison
If $f,g\gt 0$ and $\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=k$, then:
  • $0\lt k\lt\infty$: $\int f$ converges $\iff$ $\int g$ converges
  • $k=0$: $\int g$ converges $\Rightarrow$ $\int f$ converges
  • $k=\infty$: $\int f$ converges $\Rightarrow$ $\int g$ converges
Intuition
If $f/g\to k$ (finite, nonzero), $f$ and $g$ have the same growth rate at infinity — they share the same convergence fate.

Beta & Gamma Functions

Two special functions built from improper integrals. Gamma extends the factorial to all positive reals. Beta measures the "shape" of a distribution on $[0,1]$ and connects back to Gamma through a beautiful identity.

The Gamma Function

Definition — Gamma Function
$$\Gamma(n)=\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^{n-1}\,dx, \quad n\gt 0$$
Properties of Gamma
  1. $\Gamma(1)=1$
  2. Recurrence: $\Gamma(n+1)=n\Gamma(n)$
  3. Factorial: $\Gamma(n+1)=n!$ for positive integers $n$
  4. $\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)=\sqrt{\pi}$
  5. Extension: define $\Gamma(n)=\Gamma(n+1)/n$ for $-1\lt n\lt 0$, and so on for all $n\notin\{0,-1,-2,\dots\}$
Integrate by parts: $\Gamma(n+1)=\int_0^\infty x^n e^{-x}dx=[-x^ne^{-x}]_0^\infty+n\int_0^\infty x^{n-1}e^{-x}dx=n\Gamma(n)$, since $x^ne^{-x}\to 0$ as $x\to\infty$.
Theorem 0.7.1 — Convergence of Γ
For every $n\gt 0$, the improper integral $\Gamma(n)=\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^{n-1}\,dx$ converges.
Split at $x=N$. For $x\ge N$: by L'Hôpital, $x^{n-1}/e^{x/2}\to 0$, so $e^{-x}x^{n-1}\le e^{-x/2}$, and $\int_N^\infty e^{-x/2}dx\lt\infty$. For $0\lt n\lt 1$ near $0$: $e^{-x}x^{n-1}\le x^{n-1}$, and $\int_0^N x^{n-1}dx=N^n/n\lt\infty$ (since $n\gt 0$). For $n\ge 1$: no singularity at $0$.

Gamma Transformations

Form I — $x^n=t$
$\Gamma(n)=\dfrac{1}{n}\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-t^{1/n}}\,dt$

Setting $n=\frac{1}{2}$: $\;\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)=2\int_0^\infty e^{-x^2}dx=\sqrt{\pi}$

Form II — $x=kt$
$\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-kx}x^{n-1}\,dx=\dfrac{\Gamma(n)}{k^n}$

Extremely useful for evaluating integrals of the form $e^{-kx}x^{n-1}$

Form III — $e^{-x}=t$ (log form)
$\Gamma(n)=\displaystyle\int_0^1\!\left(\log\tfrac{1}{t}\right)^{n-1}dt$

Substitution $e^{-x}=t$, so $x=\log(1/t)$, $dx=-dt/t$

Form IV — $x=t^2$
$\Gamma(n)=2\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-x^2}x^{2n-1}\,dx$

Connects Gamma to Gaussian integrals

\[ \Gamma(x)\Gamma(1-x)=\frac{\pi}{\sin(\pi x)}. \]

Therefore

\[ B\!\left(\frac13,\frac23\right) = \frac{\Gamma(1/3)\Gamma(2/3)}{\Gamma(1)} = \frac{\pi}{\sin(\pi/3)} = \boxed{\frac{2\pi}{\sqrt3}}. \]

Also remember: \(\Gamma(1)=1\), \(\Gamma(2)=1\), and \(\Gamma(3)=2\).

Improper integral as Beta integral

For \(n>0\), use \(t=x^n\):

\[ \int_0^1\frac{dx}{\sqrt{1-x^n}} = \frac1n\int_0^1 t^{1/n-1}(1-t)^{-1/2}\,dt = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{n}\frac{\Gamma(1/n)}{\Gamma(1/2+1/n)}. \]
