Vectors, Lines & Planes

Vectors give direction and magnitude to geometry in two and three dimensions. Three operations — dot product, cross product, and triple product — turn geometric questions (angle, perpendicularity, area, volume) into algebra.

The three operations on vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$:

$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta \qquad |\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}| = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta$

Dot → scalar (angle). Cross → vector (perpendicular, area).

Vectors

A vector has both magnitude and direction. In component form it is a list of coordinates; geometrically it is an arrow.

Definition — Vector
A vector $\mathbf{a}=\langle a_1,a_2,a_3\rangle = a_1\mathbf{i}+a_2\mathbf{j}+a_3\mathbf{k}$ has magnitude $|\mathbf{a}|=\sqrt{a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2}$. Two vectors are equal iff they have the same magnitude and direction.
Definition — Unit Vector
A vector of magnitude 1. The unit vector in the direction of $\mathbf{a}$ is $\hat{\mathbf{a}}=\dfrac{\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{a}|}$.
Operations
$$\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}=\langle a_1+b_1,\,a_2+b_2,\,a_3+b_3\rangle \qquad k\mathbf{a}=\langle ka_1,ka_2,ka_3\rangle$$ Triangle law: place $\mathbf{b}$ at the tip of $\mathbf{a}$; the shortcut arrow is $\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}$.
Theorem — Ratio Formula
If $C$ divides $AB$ in the ratio $m:n$ (internally), then $\mathbf{c}=\dfrac{n\,\mathbf{a}+m\,\mathbf{b}}{m+n}$. Midpoint: $m=n=1$, so $\mathbf{c}=\frac{\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}}{2}$.

Dot Product

The dot product measures how much one vector points along another. Its value is a scalar. It is the engine behind angle calculations and projections.

Definition — Dot Product
$$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta = a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3$$
Theorem — Orthogonality
For non-zero vectors: $\mathbf{a}\perp\mathbf{b}\iff\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0$. This is because $\cos 90^\circ=0$.
Intuition
The dot product is zero when the vectors are perpendicular — neither "points into" the other. It is maximum when they are parallel and of the same direction ($\cos 0^\circ=1$).

Projection

The scalar projection of $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\mathbf{a}$ is $\text{comp}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b}=\dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|}$. The vector projection is $\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b}=\dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|^2}\,\mathbf{a}$.

Cross Product

The cross product of two vectors is a new vector perpendicular to both. Its magnitude equals the area of the parallelogram they span. Direction is given by the right-hand rule.

Definition — Cross Product
$$\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta\;\mathbf{n}$$ In component form via the determinant: $$\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}=\begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\end{vmatrix}=(a_2b_3-a_3b_2)\,\mathbf{i}-(a_1b_3-a_3b_1)\,\mathbf{j}+(a_1b_2-a_2b_1)\,\mathbf{k}$$
Key Properties
  1. Anti-commutativity: $\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}=-({\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{a}})$
  2. Distributivity: $\mathbf{a}\times(\mathbf{b}+\mathbf{c})=\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}+\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{c}$
  3. Parallel test: $\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{0}$ iff $\mathbf{a}\parallel\mathbf{b}$ (or either is $\mathbf{0}$)
Area Formulas
Parallelogram with adjacent sides $\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}$: $A=|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|$.  Triangle: $A=\frac12|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|$.
Intuition
When $\theta=0$ or $180^\circ$, $\sin\theta=0$: the vectors are parallel and the parallelogram has zero area. When $\theta=90^\circ$, $\sin\theta=1$: maximum area. The magnitude grows as the vectors open wider toward 90° and shrinks back to zero at 180°.

Triple Products

Combining dot and cross gives two kinds of triple product: the scalar triple product (a number equal to signed volume) and the vector triple product (a vector).

Definition — Scalar Triple Product
$$[\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c}]=\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})=\begin{vmatrix}a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\\c_1&c_2&c_3\end{vmatrix}$$
Geometric Meaning
$$\text{Volume of parallelepiped} = |\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})| \qquad \text{Volume of tetrahedron} = \frac{1}{6}|\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})|$$ Three vectors are coplanar $\iff\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})=0$.
Intuition
$\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}$ gives a vector normal to the base parallelogram with magnitude equal to its area. Dotting with $\mathbf{a}$ gives the height component — so the product is base area × height = volume.
Definition — Vector Triple Product
$$\mathbf{a}\times(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})=(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{c})\,\mathbf{b}-(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})\,\mathbf{c}$$ $$(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b})\times\mathbf{c}=(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{c})\,\mathbf{b}-(\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{c})\,\mathbf{a}$$
Common Mistake
Cross product is not associative: $\mathbf{a}\times(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})\ne(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b})\times\mathbf{c}$ in general. Brackets matter.
Linear independence test

For three vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$, form the $3\times3$ matrix $A$ with them as rows.

$$\det(A)\neq 0 \Rightarrow \text{linearly independent}\qquad \det(A)=0\Rightarrow\text{linearly dependent}$$

Geometrically, $|\det(A)|$ is the volume of the parallelepiped. Zero volume means the vectors collapse into a plane or line — exactly the condition for coplanarity.

Lines in 3D

A line in 3D is uniquely determined by one fixed point and one direction vector. The parameter $t$ moves you along the line in either direction.

Definition — Vector Equation of a Line
Line through $\mathbf{r}_0$ in direction $\mathbf{v}=\langle a,b,c\rangle$: $$\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{r}_0+t\mathbf{v}, \quad t\in\mathbb{R}$$ Parametric: $x=x_0+at,\;y=y_0+bt,\;z=z_0+ct$.   Symmetric: $\dfrac{x-x_0}{a}=\dfrac{y-y_0}{b}=\dfrac{z-z_0}{c}$.
Line Through Two Points
If the line passes through $A(\mathbf{r}_1)$ and $B(\mathbf{r}_2)$: $$\mathbf{r}=(1-t)\mathbf{r}_1+t\mathbf{r}_2$$ Direction vector is $\mathbf{r}_2-\mathbf{r}_1$.
Angle Between Two Lines
Use only the direction vectors $\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2$: $\cos\phi=\dfrac{\mathbf{v}_1\cdot\mathbf{v}_2}{|\mathbf{v}_1||\mathbf{v}_2|}$. If $\phi$ is obtuse, take $180°-\phi$ for the acute angle between the lines.
Parallel lines
Direction vectors are scalar multiples: $\mathbf{v}_0=k\,\mathbf{v}_1$.
Perpendicular lines
Direction vectors have zero dot product: $\mathbf{v}_0\cdot\mathbf{v}_1=0$.
Skew lines in 3D

Two lines are skew when they are not parallel and do not intersect. To classify two lines:

  1. Check direction vectors. If one is a scalar multiple of the other, the lines are parallel (not skew).
  2. If not parallel, solve the two parametric equations simultaneously for $t$ and $s$.
  3. If a consistent solution exists, they intersect. If not, they are skew.

Note: opposite direction vectors still mean parallel, e.g. $(-3,-4,2)=-(3,4,-2)$.

Planes

A plane is uniquely determined by one point on it plus a normal vector (perpendicular to every vector in the plane). Alternatively, give one point and two directions lying in the plane.

Definition — Normal Form
Plane through $\mathbf{r}_0$ with normal $\mathbf{n}=\langle a,b,c\rangle$: $$(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_0)\cdot\mathbf{n}=0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad ax+by+cz=d$$ where $d=ax_0+by_0+cz_0=\mathbf{r}_0\cdot\mathbf{n}$.
Definition — Parametric Form
Plane through $\mathbf{r}_0$ containing directions $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$: $$\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{r}_0+s\,\mathbf{a}+t\,\mathbf{b}$$ The normal is $\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}$.
Plane Through Three Points
For points $A,B,C$ with position vectors $\mathbf{r}_0,\mathbf{r}_1,\mathbf{r}_2$: $$(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_1)\cdot\left[(\mathbf{r}_0-\mathbf{r}_1)\times(\mathbf{r}_2-\mathbf{r}_1)\right]=0$$
Angle Between Two Planes
Use the normal vectors $\mathbf{n}_1,\mathbf{n}_2$: $\cos\phi=\dfrac{\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2}{|\mathbf{n}_1||\mathbf{n}_2|}$.

Distances

All distance formulas reduce to projecting a connecting vector onto a unit normal or unit common-perpendicular. The absolute value removes sign.

Theorem — Point to Plane
Distance from point $P(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ to plane $ax+by+cz=d$: $$D=\frac{|ax_1+by_1+cz_1-d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}$$
The unit normal to the plane is $\hat{\mathbf{n}}=\frac{\mathbf{n}}{|\mathbf{n}|}$. If $Q$ is any point on the plane, the distance from $P$ to the plane equals the length of the projection of $\overrightarrow{QP}$ onto $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$, giving $|\overrightarrow{QP}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}|=\frac{|ax_1+by_1+cz_1-d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}$.
Theorem — Distance Between Skew Lines
For lines with directions $\mathbf{v}_0,\mathbf{v}_1$ and points $A,B$ on each: $$D=\frac{|\overrightarrow{AB}\cdot(\mathbf{v}_0\times\mathbf{v}_1)|}{|\mathbf{v}_0\times\mathbf{v}_1|}$$ The numerator projects the connecting vector onto the common perpendicular.
Intuition
$\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{v}_0\times\mathbf{v}_1$ is perpendicular to both lines. The shortest connector between the skew lines must lie along this direction. So project any connector $\overrightarrow{AB}$ onto $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.
