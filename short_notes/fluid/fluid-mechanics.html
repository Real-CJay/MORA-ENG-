CE1023 Fluid Mechanics - Ordered Study Guide

Learn Fluid Mechanics in the right order.

This guide starts from what a fluid is, then builds properties, pressure, thrust, buoyancy, relative equilibrium, flow laws, momentum and fluid machinery. It is arranged in the order you would naturally learn the subject.

Learning order Symbols & terms 1. Fluids 2. Properties 3. Static pressure 4. Measurement 5. Plane thrust 6. Curved thrust 7. Buoyancy 8. Relative equilibrium 9. Flow laws 10. Momentum 11. Machinery Formula sheet

The learning path

Fluid mechanics becomes much easier if you learn it in this sequence. Every later topic uses the earlier ideas.

1. Matter that flowsFluids, continuum idea, density and viscosity.
2. Fluids at restPressure, Pascal's law, pressure variation with depth.
3. Resultant forcesHydrostatic thrust on plane and curved surfaces.
4. Floating bodiesBuoyancy, Archimedes' principle, stability and metacentre.
5. Moving containersRelative equilibrium and forced vortex.
6. Fluids in motionControl volume, continuity and Bernoulli.
7. Forces from flowMomentum equation, jets, bends and vanes.
8. MachinesPumps and turbines as energy transfer devices.
Exam mindset: first identify whether the fluid is at rest, relatively at rest, or flowing. Then choose hydrostatics, relative equilibrium, or conservation laws.

Symbols and terms used in the equations

Before solving problems, get comfortable with the symbols. Most equations in fluid mechanics are short, but every symbol carries a physical meaning.

\(\rho\)Density, mass per unit volume (kg/m³).
\(\gamma\)Specific weight, weight per unit volume; \(\gamma=\rho g\) (N/m³).
\(\mu\)Dynamic viscosity, resistance to shear flow (Pa·s).
\(\nu\)Kinematic viscosity, \(\nu=\mu/\rho\) (m²/s).
\(p\)Pressure, normal force per unit area (Pa).
\(h\), \(z\)Heights: \(h\) is usually depth below free surface; \(z\) is elevation from a datum.
\(A\), \(V\)Area and velocity. In pipes, \(Q=AV\).
\(Q\), \(\dot m\)Discharge (m³/s) and mass flow rate (kg/s), where \(\dot m=\rho Q\).
\(H\)Total head: elevation head + pressure head + velocity head.
\(F\), \(F_H\), \(F_V\), \(F_R\)Force, horizontal component, vertical component, and resultant hydrostatic force.
\(U\), \(W\)Upthrust/buoyant force and weight.
\(G\), \(B\), \(M\)Centre of gravity, centre of buoyancy, and metacentre.
\(I_G\), \(I\)Second moment of area. Used in centre of pressure and metacentric height.
\(a_x,a_y,a_z\)Container accelerations along axes in relative equilibrium.
\(\omega\), \(r\)Angular speed and radius in vortex motion.
\(H_P,H_L,H_T\)Pump head added, head loss, and turbine head removed.
Reading equations tip: whenever you see an equation, first say in words what each symbol means. This prevents memorising formulas without understanding them.

1. What is a fluid?

A fluid is a substance that continuously deforms under shear stress, however small the shear stress is. Liquids and gases are fluids. In equilibrium, there is no flow and no shear force; only normal pressure forces act.

Definition
A fluid cannot permanently resist shear deformation. A solid can resist shear; a fluid keeps changing shape while shear is applied.
StateVolumeShapeCompressibility
SolidFixedFixedLow
LiquidFixedNo fixed shapeLow
GasNo fixed volumeNo fixed shapeHigh
Important idea: in statics, pressure forces are normal to surfaces. That is why hydrostatic thrust acts perpendicular to a submerged plane surface.
Solid resists shear; liquid and gas flow under shear.

2. Fluid properties you must know first

These properties are the language used in every equation after this point.

Density
\(\rho=\dfrac{m}{V}\)
Units: kg/m³
Specific weight
\(\gamma=\dfrac{W}{V}=\rho g\)
Units: N/m³
Relative density
\(S=\dfrac{\rho}{\rho_w}\)
Dimensionless
Bulk modulus
\(K=-\dfrac{dp}{dV/V}=\rho\dfrac{dp}{d\rho}\)
High K = low compressibility
Surface tension
\(\sigma=\dfrac{F}{L}\)
Important in capillarity/small scale
Kinematic viscosity
\(\nu=\dfrac{\mu}{\rho}\)
Units: m²/s

Newton's law of viscosity

Viscous shear law
\[\tau=\mu\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\]
Valid for Newtonian fluids: water, air, many common oils under usual conditions.
Physical meaning If one layer of fluid moves faster than the neighboring layer, internal friction appears between the layers. The stronger the velocity gradient, the larger the shear stress.
Viscosity connects shear stress to velocity gradient.

3. Static fluid pressure

Now we enter fluid statics: the study of fluids in equilibrium. No flow means no shear stress, so pressure acts normally and equally in all directions at a point.

Pascal's law
The hydrostatic pressure at a point is the same in all directions.
Pressure definition
\[p=\frac{F}{A}\]
Pressure is a scalar, but the force caused by pressure is normal to the surface.

Hydrostatic pressure variation

Fluid at rest
\[\frac{dp}{dz}=-\rho g\] \[p_2-p_1=\rho g h=\gamma h\]

For a free surface open to the atmosphere, using gauge pressure:

Depth h below free surface
\[p=\rho gh=\gamma h\]
\(p\)Gauge pressure at the point.
\(\rho\)Density of the liquid.
\(g\)Acceleration due to gravity.
\(h\)Vertical depth below free surface.
\(\gamma\)Specific weight of the liquid, \(\rho g\).
At a point pressure is equal in all directions; with depth it increases linearly.
Absolute, gauge and vacuum pressure
Absolute pressure
Measured from absolute vacuum.
\(p_{abs}=p_{atm}+p_g\)
Gauge pressure
Measured relative to atmosphere.
\(p_g=p_{abs}-p_{atm}\)
Vacuum pressure
When pressure is below atmospheric.
\(p_{vac}=p_{atm}-p_{abs}\)
Pressure head
Pressure expressed as liquid column height.
\(h=p/\gamma\)

4. Measuring pressure: heads, piezometers and manometers

Once pressure is understood, measurement devices are only applications of the hydrostatic pressure law.

Piezometric head

Piezometric head
\[H_p=z+\frac{p}{\gamma}\]

This is elevation head plus pressure head. It does not include velocity head.

Differential manometer rule

For two points A and B in the same liquid connected by a heavier manometric liquid:

Common mercury differential manometer result
\[\left(\frac{p_A}{\gamma_w}+z_A\right)-\left(\frac{p_B}{\gamma_w}+z_B\right)=(S_m-1)h\]
The \((S_m-1)\) appears because the manometer liquid replaces the same height of pipe liquid. It is not just mercury head; it is excess head over water.
Piezometers measure pressure head directly; differential manometers compare two piezometric heads.

5. Hydrostatic thrust on plane surfaces

After pressure, the next question is: what is the single resultant force on a whole submerged surface?

Hydrostatic thrust
The resultant force exerted by a fluid in equilibrium on a submerged surface. Direction is normal to the plane surface.

Magnitude

General plane surface
\[F=\int_A p\,dA\] \[F=\rho g A \bar h=A p_G\]
\(\bar h\) is the vertical depth of the centroid. \(p_G\) is pressure at the centroid.

Line of action / centre of pressure

Distance along inclined plane from free surface
\[y_C=\bar y+\frac{I_G}{A\bar y}\] \[h_C=y_C\sin\theta\]
Why centre of pressure is below centroid: deeper parts have larger pressure, so the resultant shifts toward the deeper side. Therefore usually \(y_C>\bar y\).
\(F\)Resultant hydrostatic thrust on the whole surface.
\(A\)Submerged area of the plane surface.
\(\bar h\)Vertical depth of the centroid \(G\).
\(p_G\)Pressure at the centroid of the area.
\(\bar y\), \(y_C\)Distances along the plane to centroid and centre of pressure.
\(I_G\)Second moment of area about centroidal axis parallel to free surface.
The resultant acts normal to the surface and below the centroid.
Pressure diagram shortcut For vertical rectangular surfaces, the pressure diagram is triangular if it starts at the free surface and trapezoidal if the top is already submerged. The thrust per unit width is the area of this pressure diagram. The line of action passes through the centroid of the pressure diagram.

6. Hydrostatic thrust on curved surfaces

For curved surfaces, pressure forces are normal at every point, so the resultant is found by components.

Curved surface components
\[F_H=\text{force on vertical projection}\] \[F_V=\text{weight of imaginary liquid above surface}\] \[F_R=\sqrt{F_H^2+F_V^2}\] \[\tan\theta=\frac{F_V}{F_H}\]
Procedure: take the horizontal component using the vertical projected area. Take the vertical component using the weight of water directly above the curved surface, including sign depending on whether water is above or below the surface.
Resolve a curved-surface force into horizontal and vertical components.

7. Buoyancy, floating and stability

Buoyancy is hydrostatic thrust integrated over the whole body. The horizontal components cancel; the vertical resultant is the upthrust.

Archimedes' principle
The buoyant force on a body equals the weight of the displaced fluid and acts upward through the centre of buoyancy.
Buoyancy
\[U=\rho g V_{disp}=\gamma V_{disp}\]

Equilibrium conditions

ConditionMeaning
\(U=W\)Vertical equilibrium
\(U>W\)Body rises
\(UBody sinks

Two immiscible fluids

Total upthrust
\[U=V_1\rho_1g+V_2\rho_2g\]
\(U\)Buoyant force (upthrust).
\(V_{disp}\)Volume of displaced fluid.
\(W\)Weight of the body.
\(B, G, M\)Centre of buoyancy, centre of gravity, and metacentre.
\(GM\)Metacentric height; positive for stable floating equilibrium.
Buoyancy acts through B. For floating stability, use the metacentre M.

Fully submerged stability

Relative positionEquilibrium
\(B\) above \(G\)Stable
\(B\) below \(G\)Unstable
\(B\equiv G\)Neutral
Small angle restoring moment
\[M_R=W(GB)\sin\theta\approx W(GB)\theta\]

Floating body stability

Metacentric conditionEquilibrium
\(GM>0\)Stable
\(GM<0\)Unstable
\(GM=0\)Neutral
Metacentric height
\[BM=\frac{I}{V}\qquad GM=BM-BG\]\[M_R=W(GM)\sin\theta\]
Experimental metacentric height Move a known weight \(P\) by distance \(x\), measure small tilt angle \(\theta\): \[GM=\frac{Px}{W\tan\theta}\approx\frac{Px}{W\theta}\]
Free surface effect Liquid cargo can reduce stability because the liquid shifts during tilt. Effective metacentric height decreases: \[GM_{effective}=GM-GN\] Splitting the liquid into smaller compartments reduces this effect.

8. Relative equilibrium of fluids

A fluid in a moving container may still be at rest relative to the container. Since there is no relative motion between fluid particles, fluid statics can still be used.

Linear acceleration

Pressure field in accelerating container
\[dp=-\rho a_xdx-\rho a_ydy-\rho(a_z+g)dz\] \[p=-\rho a_xx-\rho a_yy-\rho(a_z+g)z+C\]
Free surface
\[\rho a_xx+\rho a_yy+\rho(a_z+g)z=C\] \[\tan\theta_x=\frac{dz}{dx}=-\frac{a_x}{a_z+g}\]
Special case: if acceleration is horizontal only, the magnitude of the free-surface slope is \(\tan\theta=a/g\).
\(a_x,a_y,a_z\)Acceleration components of the container.
\(C\)Integration constant defining the free-surface plane.
\(\theta\)Inclination of the free surface.
Relative equilibrium under horizontal acceleration.

Forced vortex motion

If a vessel rotates at constant angular velocity, the fluid rotates with it like a rigid body.

Forced vortex
\[v=r\omega\] \[\frac{\partial p}{\partial r}=\rho r\omega^2\] \[p=\frac{\rho\omega^2r^2}{2}-\rho gz+C\] \[z=z_0+\frac{\omega^2r^2}{2g}\]
In a forced vortex, the free surface is parabolic.

9. Fluids in motion: control volume, mass and energy

Now the fluid is flowing. We stop tracking every particle and use a control volume: a real or imaginary region through which fluid flows.

Control volume idea

Conservation laws are applied to flow through the control volume: mass, energy and momentum.

Conservation of mass

Continuity equation
\[\dot m_{in}-\dot m_{out}=\frac{dm_{CV}}{dt}\]
Steady incompressible single-inlet/single-outlet flow:
\[Q=A V=\text{constant}\] \[A_1V_1=A_2V_2\]

Bernoulli and total head

Total head
\[H=z+\frac{p}{\rho g}+\frac{V^2}{2g}\] \[z_1+\frac{p_1}{\rho g}+\frac{V_1^2}{2g}=z_2+\frac{p_2}{\rho g}+\frac{V_2^2}{2g}\]
Steady flow energy equation
\[H_1+H_P=H_2+H_L+H_T\]
Pump adds head; turbine removes head; losses consume head.
\(Q\)Discharge or volume flow rate.
\(A, V\)Flow area and mean velocity.
\(z\)Elevation head measured from a datum.
\(p/(\rho g)\)Pressure head.
\(V^2/(2g)\)Velocity head.
\(H_P,H_L,H_T\)Pump head added, loss head, and turbine head extracted.
Continuity links area and velocity; Bernoulli links pressure, speed and elevation.
When can Bernoulli be used directly? Use it for steady, incompressible, frictionless flow along a streamline. In real pipe problems, extend it using head loss and pump/turbine head.

10. Conservation of momentum

Energy tells you pressure/speed/head. Momentum tells you force. Use it for jets, pipe bends, nozzles, vanes and propellers.

Newton's second law for flow
The net external force on a control volume equals the rate of change of momentum of the fluid.
Steady flow momentum equation
\[\sum F=\dot m(V_{out}-V_{in})\] \[\sum F_x=\rho Q(V_{2x}-V_{1x})\] \[\sum F_y=\rho Q(V_{2y}-V_{1y})\]
Sign warning: this gives force on the fluid. The force of the fluid on the pipe/vane is equal and opposite.
\(\sum F\)Net external force acting on the chosen control volume.
\(\dot m\)Mass flow rate of fluid through the control volume.
\(V_{1x},V_{2x}\)x-components of inlet and outlet velocity.
\(V_{1y},V_{2y}\)y-components of inlet and outlet velocity.
\(R_x,R_y\)Reaction components usually solved from momentum balance.

Momentum problem checklist

  1. Draw a control volume.
  2. Resolve inlet and outlet velocities into components.
  3. Show pressure forces, weight if needed, and reactions.
  4. Apply continuity to get \(Q\) or velocities.
  5. Apply momentum in x and y separately.
Velocity direction changes, so momentum changes, so a force is required.

11. Fluid machinery: pumps and turbines

Fluid machines transfer energy between a moving/rotating machine part and a fluid.

Pumps

Energy direction: machine → fluid. A pump adds head to the liquid.
Pump head and power
\[H_P=H_2-H_1+H_L\] \[P_w=\rho gQH_P\] \[\eta_p=\frac{\rho gQH_P}{P_{input}}\]
\(H_P\)Head added by the pump.
\(P_w\)Water power transferred to the fluid.
\(\eta_p\)Pump efficiency.
\(Q\)Discharge through the machine.
ArrangementEffect
SeriesHeads add: \(H_T=H_1+H_2\)
ParallelDischarges add: \(Q_T=Q_1+Q_2\)

Turbines

Energy direction: fluid → machine. A turbine extracts head from flowing water.
Turbine power
\[P_h=\rho gQH\] \[P_{out}=\eta_t\rho gQH\]
\(P_h\)Hydraulic power available in the fluid.
\(P_{out}\)Useful shaft power output.
\(\eta_t\)Turbine efficiency.
\(H\)Head available across the turbine.
TypeTypical idea
ImpulseJet strikes buckets; high head applications.
ReactionPressure changes through runner; Francis/Kaplan examples.
Pumps and turbines are opposite energy-transfer devices.
Positive displacement vs rotodynamic machines Positive displacement machines trap fluid in an enclosed volume and force it out by mechanical action. Rotodynamic machines exchange energy between a flowing fluid and a rotating impeller, runner or rotor.
