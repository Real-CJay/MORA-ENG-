01 - Foundations

Nanoscale, nanomaterials, and why nano matters

01

Nanoscale

The nanoscale is approximately 1 nm to 100 nm. One nanometer is 10-9 m.

Nanomaterial
A material is a nanomaterial if it has either an external dimension in the nanoscale, or internal/surface structure in the nanoscale.

Nano-object vs nanostructured material

TermMeaningExamples
Nano-objectA discrete object with at least one external dimension in the nanoscale.CNT, buckyball, nanoparticle, nanowire
Nanostructured materialA larger material whose internal structure or surface structure is nanoscale.TiO₂ nanotube thin film, nanoporous surface

A nano-object is one nanoscale thing; a nanostructured material may be larger but contains nanoscale features.

Why nanomaterials have unusual properties

Extra knowledgeThese are common reasons used to explain the lecture idea that nanoscale materials behave differently from bulk materials.

Surface-to-volume

Smaller particles expose more atoms at the surface, so surface reactions, wetting, catalysis, and adhesion become stronger.

Quantum/size effects

At nanoscale, allowed electronic states can change. This is why size can influence band gap and conductivity.

Defect + structure control

Nanostructure, crystallinity, defects, and interfaces can dominate mechanical, electrical, and thermal behaviour.

Lecture examples: graphene has much higher Young's modulus than graphite, and single-layer graphene can have much higher carrier mobility than bulk metals. The point is not just small size; it is the changed structure and physics at nanoscale.

Nano in nature

Lotus effect

Lotus leaves are superhydrophobic because micro/nano surface roughness traps air and reduces wetting. Contact angle greater than 90° means hydrophobic; less than 90° means hydrophilic.

Gecko adhesive system

Gecko feet show hierarchical structure from macro to meso to micro to nanostructures. Many tiny spatula-like tips create strong adhesion through surface interactions.

02 - Fabrication routes

How nanomaterials are prepared

02
Top-down approach

Start with larger material and create nanoscale features by removing, cutting, patterning, or exfoliating.

bulk material→pattern/remove/peel→nano features

Examples: lithography, etching, mechanical exfoliation, chemical exfoliation.

Bottom-up approach

Start from atoms, ions, molecules, or gaseous precursors and build the nanomaterial by assembly or growth.

atoms/molecules→nucleation→nanostructure

Examples: molecular self-assembly, CVD.

Photolithography + etching

Photolithography creates tiny patterns on silicon or thin films using a photosensitive material and a mask. After development, etching transfers the pattern into the substrate.

  1. Coat Si wafer with photoresist.
  2. Expose through a mask using radiation.
  3. Develop to remove exposed or unexposed regions.
  4. Etch the wafer where material is not protected.
  5. Remove the remaining photoresist.
Etching = selective removal. It is like microscopic carving: protected regions remain, exposed regions are removed chemically or by plasma.

Lithography defines the pattern; etching transfers the pattern.

Chemical vapor deposition (CVD)

In CVD, gaseous precursors react on a heated substrate to grow a solid coating or nanostructure.

CVD is a bottom-up route because atoms/molecules from the gas phase add to the surface and grow a film or nanostructure. In graphene CVD, a carbon-containing gas such as methane can decompose on a catalytic surface; carbon nucleates and grows into graphene.

CH₄ → C(graphene) + 2H₂

Think of CVD as atomic construction, not carving.

03 - Carbon-based nanomaterials

Graphene: structure, preparation, and properties

03

What is graphene?

Graphene is a 2D crystal of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal network. It is one atomic layer of graphite.

Carbon allotropes discussed in the lecture include graphite, diamond, buckminsterfullerene, CNTs, and graphene.

Graphite vs graphene: graphite = many stacked graphene layers; graphene = one sheet.

The hexagons show C-C bonding. The black rhombuses align precisely to equivalent sub-lattice atoms, tracing the proper primitive translation unit cells.

Graphene unit cell and lattice parameter

If C-C bond length is d:
a = 2d cos 30° = √3 d

Here a = graphene lattice constant, not the C-C bond length.
Common confusion
The honeycomb hexagon can be used to count 2 atoms because 6 corner atoms are shared among 3 hexagons: 6 × 1/3 = 2. But the standard primitive unit cell is the rhombus formed by the primitive translation vectors.

Graphene preparation methods

RouteMethodMain ideaStrengths / limitations
Top-downMicromechanical cleavagePeel graphene layers from graphite using tape.High-quality flakes, but labor-intensive and not scalable.
Top-downUltrasonicationGraphite dispersed in solvent is exfoliated by liquid cavitation.Easy and scalable, but low concentration, defects, fragmentation.
Top-downHummers methodChemically oxidize graphite to graphene oxide.Scalable and simple, but toxic gases, damaged basal plane, lower conductivity.
Top-down + treatmentReduction of GORemove oxygen from graphene oxide by chemical or thermal reduction.Forms rGO, but oxygen/defects are not fully removed.

Graphene properties from the lecture

Mobility

Charge carrier mobility: ~200,000 cm²/V·s

Thermal

Thermal conductivity: ~5000 W/m·K

Optical

Transparency: ~97.4%

Surface area

Specific surface area: ~2630 m²/g

Mechanical

Young's modulus: ~1 TPa; tensile strength: ~1100 GPa

Band gap

Zero band gap for ideal graphene.

04 - Carbon nanotubes

CNT structure, chirality, diameter, and properties

04

What is a CNT?

A carbon nanotube is a rolled-up sheet of graphene. The way the graphene is rolled determines the nanotube's chirality and many of its properties.

SWNT
single-walled carbon nanotube
MWNT
multi-walled carbon nanotube; inter-tube distance ~3.4 Å, similar to graphite interlayer spacing.

The chiral vector Cₕ becomes the circumference of the nanotube.

Chiral vector and (n,m) notation

Ch = n a1 + m a2
The resulting nanotube is called an (n,m) nanotube.

The chiral vector must connect equivalent atoms in graphene. Near-neighbor atoms are not equivalent; next-near neighbors are equivalent. That is why Cₕ is written using graphene primitive vectors.

Armchair

(n,n)

Achiral; has mirror plane; chiral angle θ = 30°. Example: (4,4).

Zigzag

(n,0)

Achiral; has mirror plane; chiral angle θ = 0°. Example: (5,0).

Chiral

(n,m), n ≠ m, m ≠ 0

No mirror plane; helical arrangement; 0° < θ < 30°. Example: (4,3).

Visual guide: armchair, zigzag, chiral

Chiral angle

θ = tan-1 [ √3 m / (2n + m) ]

Zigzag: θ = 0°
Armchair: θ = 30°
Chiral: 0° < θ < 30°
Meaning: the chiral angle measures how much the tube is twisted away from the zigzag direction. It is not just a drawing label; it affects electronic structure and properties.

CNT diameter

The circumference of the CNT is the magnitude of the chiral vector:

|Ch| = a √(n² + m² + nm)
D = |Ch| / π = a/π √(n² + m² + nm)

Here a is the graphene lattice constant. If the C-C bond length is d, then a = √3 d.

Electrical properties and band gap

ConditionLecture classificationMeaning
n = mMetallicArmchair tubes are metallic in the simple model.
n ≠ m and n - m = 3qSmall band gap semiconductorAllowed electronic states nearly meet the graphene K-point condition.
OtherwiseLarge band gap semiconductorAllowed states miss the K-point condition more strongly.
Extra knowledgeBand gap intuition: for a single CNT, band gap is not mainly about surface area or “more current”. It comes from allowed electron states after rolling graphene. As radius increases, allowed states become closer together, so the band gap decreases.
Extra knowledge: K points and allowed k-lines

This goes beyond the lecture wording but helps explain the chirality rule. Graphene has K points where valence and conduction bands meet. When graphene is rolled into a CNT, electron waves around the circumference must fit exactly, so only selected k-lines are allowed. If an allowed line hits K, the CNT is metallic. If it misses K, a band gap appears. A larger diameter gives closer k-line spacing, so the miss is smaller and the band gap decreases.

CNT mechanical and thermal properties

Mechanical

Young's modulus ~0.9-1.1 TPa; max strain around 10%; very high strength-to-weight ratio.

Electrical

Metallic and semiconducting behaviour depends on chirality and size. Room-temperature conductivity differs strongly between metallic and semiconducting CNTs.

Thermal

Heat is mainly carried by phonons. Axial thermal conductivity can be very high due to strong C-C covalent bonds; radial direction is insulating.

05 - Applications

Where nanomaterials are used

05

Electronics

Nanoscale transistors and CNT transistors can enable smaller, faster, and more energy-efficient devices.

Magnetic data storage

Nanoscale magnetic domains allow high-density, ultra-compact data storage.

Energy storage

Nanoparticles, nanowires, nanorods, graphene, and CNT electrodes can improve charge storage in batteries and supercapacitors.

Surface engineering

Superhydrophobic surfaces can be made using nanoscale roughness, inspired by lotus leaves and CNT forests.

Composites

Graphene and CNTs can improve strength, conductivity, and functional behaviour in composites.

Energy harvesting

CNTs can support photovoltaic cells because of strong photoabsorption, high carrier mobility, and short carrier transport lengths.

Photovoltaic cell and CNT energy harvesting

Photon absorption creates electron-hole pairs; the depletion-region field separates them.

Extra knowledgeWhere are electron-hole pairs generated?

They are generated wherever photons are absorbed, not only in the depletion region. In a p-n cell this can be in the n-type region, p-type region, or depletion region.

The depletion region is special because its internal electric field separates electrons and holes before recombination.

light absorbed→electron-hole pair→field separates charges→current

Why CNTs help

  • Strong photoabsorption from IR to UV.
  • High carrier mobility.
  • Shorter carrier transport lengths in nanostructured junctions.

In SWNT/n-Si heterojunction cells, the useful separation mainly happens near the CNT-silicon interface where the built-in field exists.

Formula and concept sheet

06

Graphene

  • Graphene = 2D carbon hexagonal network.
  • Primitive unit cell shape = rhombus/parallelogram.
  • Atoms per unit cell = 2.
  • If C-C bond length = d, lattice parameter a = 2d cos30° = √3d.
  • Ideal graphene band gap = zero.

CNT

  • Chiral vector: Ch = n a1 + m a2.
  • Armchair: (n,n), θ = 30°.
  • Zigzag: (n,0), θ = 0°.
  • Chiral: n ≠ m, m ≠ 0, 0° < θ < 30°.
  • Diameter: D = a/π √(n² + m² + nm).
  • Chiral angle: θ = tan⁻¹(√3m/(2n+m)).

07 - Active recall

Quick checks before an exam

07
Q1. Why is the graphene primitive unit cell a rhombus, not the obvious hexagon?

Answer: The rhombus is formed by primitive translation vectors and tiles the lattice by translation. The hexagon shows bonding and can count 2 atoms, but it is not the standard primitive translation cell.
Q2. Is 1/4 atom contribution related to 3 bonds in graphene?

Answer: No. Bonding tells coordination number. Fractional contribution tells how many unit cells share a boundary atom.
Q3. Why does CNT band gap decrease as radius increases?

Answer: Larger circumference gives more closely spaced allowed electronic states; they come closer to the graphene K point condition, reducing the band gap.
Q4. Where are electron-hole pairs generated in a photovoltaic cell?

Answer: Wherever photons are absorbed. The depletion region is important because its electric field separates the carriers efficiently.
