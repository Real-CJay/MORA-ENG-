01

Introduction

Corrosion is the deterioration of metals through reaction with the environment. It is not random — it is thermodynamics. Metallic iron is an artificial high-energy state; rust (Fe₂O₃) is the natural low-energy state iron wants to occupy. All corrosion reactions are electrochemical — always a paired oxidation and reduction with electron transfer between them.

Oxidation — Anode

M → Mⁿ⁺ + ne⁻
Metal dissolves. Electrons released into the metal. This is where structural damage occurs.

Reduction — Cathode

Xⁿ⁺ + ne⁻ → X
Electrons consumed here. No metal loss. Two main reactions: HER and ORR.

HER — Acidic environments

2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂↑
Acid pickling, oil wells, food cans.

ORR — Neutral / Alkaline

O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻
Seawater, atmosphere, soil.

02

Electrochemical Series

Every metal has a standard electrode potential E⁰, measured against the Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE = 0.000 V). The more negative the E⁰, the more the metal wants to oxidise — it becomes the anode.

Cell potential — spontaneous if positive ΔE⁰ = E⁰(cathode) − E⁰(anode)
Reaction E⁰ (V) Notes
Au³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Au+1.420Most noble — never corrodes
O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O+1.229
O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻+0.401ORR in neutral water
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu+0.340Noble, cathodic to Fe
2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂0.000SHE reference
Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Fe−0.440Corrodes in most environments
Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Zn−0.763Sacrificial anode vs. Fe
Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al−1.662Active but self-passivates
Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Mg−2.363Most active — sacrificial anode

Worked example — Fe in atmosphere: ΔE⁰ = 0.401 − (−0.440) = +0.841 V. Positive → spontaneous → Fe rusts. The atmosphere (ORR) is the cathode; Fe is the anode.

Galvanic Series vs. EMF Series

The EMF series is measured under idealised laboratory conditions. The galvanic series measures actual corrosion potentials of real alloys in a specific electrolyte — usually seawater. Rankings can reverse completely in a different environment.

Key example — Tin vs. Steel: In seawater, tin is noble (cathodic to steel). Inside an acidic food can, this reverses — tin becomes anodic, protecting steel. Using the wrong series gives the wrong prediction.

03

Forms of Corrosion

  • Uniform / General — Even thinning across the whole surface. Predictable, manageable. ~30% of failures.
  • Galvanic — Two dissimilar metals in contact. Less noble metal is the anode, corrodes preferentially.
  • Crevice — Differential aeration in a narrow gap. Autocatalytic — becomes self-sustaining and accelerates.
  • Pitting — Localised breakdown of passive film. Deep holes with small surface area. Dangerous and hard to detect.
  • Intergranular — Attack along grain boundaries. Alloy loses structural cohesion while looking intact.
  • Erosion corrosion — Fast-flowing fluid + corrosive environment. Protective films stripped mechanically.
  • Stress corrosion — Tensile stress + corrosive environment → brittle cracking at stresses well below yield.
  • Selective leaching — One element preferentially removed from alloy (e.g. Zn from brass = dezincification).

Galvanic Corrosion — The Area Effect

The corrosion current density at the anode = total current ÷ anode area. Same total current concentrated on a smaller surface → higher rate of metal loss.

Dangerous — Small anode, large cathode

All current density concentrated on a tiny area. Rapid, deep pitting. Example: Al rivets in a steel panel.

Safer — Large anode, small cathode

Same current spread over large surface. Slow, uniform loss. Example: steel rivets in an Al sheet.

Design rule: always make the more active (anodic) metal the larger component in any dissimilar-metal joint.

Stainless steel warning: Stainless relies on Cr₂O₃ passive film for protection. Crevice conditions (low pH + high Cl⁻) destroy this film. Once depassivated, stainless can corrode faster than mild steel.

Differential Aeration — Oxygen Concentration Cell

A single metal in contact with electrolyte at two different oxygen concentrations forms its own galvanic cell. No second metal needed. The region with less oxygen is forced to become the anode.

Above waterline — Cathode

Open air → unlimited O₂. Thin moisture film allows ORR. Electrons consumed here. No metal loss.

Below waterline — Anode

Low dissolved O₂. Cannot sustain ORR. Forced to supply electrons. Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻. Metal dissolves here.

Two current paths form a complete circuit. Both must exist for corrosion to continue:

Path 1 — Through the metal

Electrons flow upward from anode (submerged) to cathode (above waterline). Like current in a wire.

Path 2 — Through the water

Fe²⁺ exits anode, drifts outward. OH⁻ produced at cathode drifts downward through water. Ionic current closes the loop.

Rust appears at the waterline — not where metal is lost (below), but where Fe²⁺ rising from the anode meets OH⁻ descending from the cathode and precipitates as Fe(OH)₂ → Fe₂O₃·H₂O. The visible damage and the structural damage are at different locations.

Atomic Scale
04

Corrosion from Atomic Principles

Why Iron Wants to Dissolve

Iron's two 4s electrons sit far from the nucleus, poorly shielded, loosely held. Water molecules are polar — their negative oxygen end pulls at surface Fe atoms. When the hydration energy (released as water stabilises Fe²⁺ in solution) exceeds the lattice binding energy, the atom leaves as Fe²⁺. Two electrons remain in the metal.

The thermodynamic drive to dissolve is always present. What controls corrosion rate is not whether iron wants to dissolve — it always does — but whether the electrons left behind can be efficiently removed by a cathodic reaction.

Anode and Cathode Sites on a Single Metal

A theoretically perfect pure single crystal would corrode nearly uniformly and extremely slowly. Real metal corrodes because imperfections create local potential differences:

  • Grain boundaries — Misaligned atoms, higher energy → anodic. Corrode first.
  • Dislocations — Stressed lattice atoms easier to dissolve → anodic.
  • Fe₃C inclusions — More noble than Fe matrix → cathodic. Drive dissolution of surrounding Fe.
  • MnS inclusions — Less noble → anodic. Classic pitting initiation sites in steel.
  • Residual stress — Welds, cold-worked zones → higher energy → anodic.

Why Rust Doesn't Protect — Pilling-Bedworth Ratio

When Fe oxidises, the oxide product occupies more volume than the metal it replaces. This expansion cracks and flakes the oxide layer, continuously exposing fresh metal. Contrast with aluminium, whose oxide fits perfectly and seals the surface.

Iron — PBR 2.1

Oxide 2.1× larger than metal. Cracks on formation. Never protective. Corrosion self-sustains indefinitely.

Aluminium — PBR 1.28

Oxide fits neatly. Dense, adherent Al₂O₃ film. Self-limiting. Stops after thin layer forms.

Sodium — PBR 0.57

Oxide too thin to cover surface. No protection. Reacts violently with water directly.

Where OH⁻ Comes From at the Cathode

OH⁻ is not pre-existing — it is synthesised at the reduction site from O₂ and local water molecules. O₂ adsorbs on the metal surface, receives electrons tunnelling from the metal, O=O bond breaks, each oxygen picks up H from a nearby water molecule, two OH⁻ detach into solution. The water molecule is actively broken apart in this process.

Complete breakdown at the cathode surface O₂ adsorbs on metal surface + electrons from metal (via anode) + H from local H₂O molecules ─────────────────────────────── → 4OH⁻ released into solution
Prevention Strategies
05

Prevention of Corrosion

Every prevention strategy attacks one of the three requirements for corrosion: the anodic reaction, the cathodic reaction, or the electrical/ionic path between them.

Material Selection

  • Noble metals (Au, Pt) — Immune. Impractical for general use.
  • Pure metals — Fewer galvanic micro-sites. Industrial purity insufficient; trace impurities always create local cells.
  • Corrosion-resistant alloys — Stainless steel, titanium, nickel alloys. Rely on passive oxide films.
  • Non-metals — Polymers and ceramics. Immune to electrochemical corrosion entirely.

Cathodic Protection

Make the structure the cathode — it cannot corrode if it is not the anode. Two methods:

Sacrificial Anode

Connect a more active metal (Mg, Zn, Al) to the structure. It corrodes deliberately. No external power needed. Used on ship hulls, buried pipelines, water boilers, offshore structures.

Impressed Current (ICCP)

External DC source pumps electrons into the structure, opposing the corrosion current. Uses an inert anode (graphite, mixed metal oxide). Used on large structures: oil platforms, pipelines, reinforced concrete.

The logic: a corroding metal gives away electrons (M → Mⁿ⁺ + ne⁻). Supply those electrons externally and the reaction is suppressed — the metal cannot dissolve if it already has more electrons than it wants to release.

Corrosion Inhibitors

Chemicals added to the electrolyte that slow or stop the cathodic reaction. Must be matched to the active cathodic mechanism — wrong inhibitor = no protection.

HER Poisons — acid environments

Adsorb on metal surface, blocking active H⁺ reduction sites physically.

Examples: As, Sb, Amines, Mercaptans, Thiourea

O₂ Scavengers — aerated systems

React with dissolved O₂ in bulk solution before it reaches the surface.

Examples: Hydrazine (N₂H₄), Na₂SO₃, Carbohydrazide

Protective Coatings

Interrupt the ionic path between metal and electrolyte. Without electrolyte contact, the circuit cannot be completed and corrosion halts.

Metallic — Galvanising

Zinc on steel. Barrier + sacrificial: if scratched, Zn is still anodic to Fe and continues to protect it electrochemically.

Inorganic Non-metallic

Ceramic, phosphate, chromate layers. Chemically inert. Used in high-temperature and aggressive chemical environments.

Organic — Paints

Pigment in drying oil binder. Anti-corrosion paints incorporate inhibitors (zinc chromate primers). Binder must be impermeable.

Zinc vs. Tin on steel: Zn is anodic to Fe — scratched coating still protects galvanically. Tin is cathodic to Fe — a scratched tin coating accelerates corrosion of steel beneath. This is why zinc coating (galvanising) is strongly preferred.

Other Materials
06

Degradation of Polymers

Unlike metal corrosion (electrochemical), polymer degradation is physiochemical — involving bond rupture, chain scission, swelling, and dissolution. The result is a decrease in molecular weight and a loss of mechanical properties.

Agents of Degradation

Heat — Breaks backbone bonds. Hydrocarbon polymers especially susceptible.

UV / Radiation — Excites aromatic groups. Embrittlement, yellowing.

Chemicals — Hydrolysis of ester, amide, urethane linkages.

Stress — Crazing, stress cracking, fatigue.

Swelling and Dissolution

Solvent molecules diffuse between chains, forcing them apart. Secondary bonds (van der Waals, H-bonds) weaken. Polymer softens and swells.

Continued: chains separate completely → dissolution. Cross-linked polymers resist dissolution but still swell.

Degradation is undesirable during service life but deliberately engineered afterward — biodegradable packaging uses the same chain-scission principles applied at a controlled rate.

07

Corrosion of Ceramics

Ceramics are already in their oxidised, lowest-energy state — ionically or covalently bonded oxides, nitrides, carbides. There is no thermodynamic drive to oxidise further. This is precisely why ceramics are used where metals would corrode.

Strengths

Resistant to most acids, alkalis, and salt solutions. Glass withstands nearly all common acids. Refractory ceramics are stable at temperatures that would oxidise or melt metals.

Weaknesses

Strong alkalis attack silicate glass (Si–O bond hydrolysis). HF dissolves glass uniquely. Thermal shock causes cracking. Some ceramics are soluble in specific concentrated acid environments.
